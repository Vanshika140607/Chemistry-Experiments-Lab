Chemistry Experiments

1. Title

To determine the chloride(Cl-) content of water sample by Mohrs Method (Argentometric method).

2. Prior Concept

Volumetric analysis,Indicator theory.

New Concepts

Precipitation titration,chemical reactions.

Proposition 1

Chlorides are present in water usually as NaCl, MgCl2, and CaCl2. Chlorides are not harmful, but their concentrations above 250 ppm impart a peculiar taste to the water, thus making the water unacceptable for drinking purposes. High concentration of chloride in the water sample indicates pollution. MgCl2 and CaCl2 significantly increase the Cl- content.

Proposition 2

Mohr's method is used for the determination of chloride ions in the water sample, which is neutral or slightly alkaline. This method determines the chloride ion concentration of a solution by titration with silver nitrate. As the silver nitrate solution is slowly added, a precipitate of silver chloride forms.

The indicator used is dilute potassium chromate solution. When all the chloride ions have reacted, any excess silver nitrate added will react with chromate ions to form a red-brown precipitate of silver chromate. This procedure is known as Mohr's method.

The pH of the water sample should be between 7 to 8.5. At pH higher than 8.5, silver ions get hydrated to form the silver hydroxide, and at the pH below 7(acidic), potassium chromate is converted to potassium dichromate.

4. Chemical Reaction

Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl (white precipitate)

2AgNO3(aq) + K2CrO₄(aq) → Ag₂CrO₄(s) +2KNO3(reddish-brown precipitate)

5. Chemicals

Standard 0.02N silver nitrate solution and 5% potassium chromate solution as indicator.

6. Apparatus

No instruments required.

9. Procedure

  • Standard 0.02 normal silver nitrate solution, dissolve exactly 3.3978 gram of dry solid AgNO3 in distilled water and dilute it to 1 liter with distilled water in a volumetric flask, then store in a brown bottle.
  • 5% potassium chromate solution dissolve 5 gram of solid K2CrO4 in 100 ml of distilled water
  • Determination of chloride content.
  • Back titration:

    • Pipette out 50 ml of given water sample into a 250 ml of conical flask and add 5 to 6 drops of potassium chromate as indicator. Now titrate this yellow coloured solution against standard 0.02N silver nitrate solution taken in burette. Initially, white precipitate of silver chloride is formed which appears yellow due to the indicator. When all the chloride ions in water sample gets precipitated, then addition of an extra drop of silver nitrate forms a reddish-brown precipitate of silver chromate. Thus, formation of reddish-brown precipitate indicates the end point. Note down the burette reading and repeat the same titration for two more times. Let the constant burette reading be x ml.

  • Blank titration:

    • The blank titration is also carried out as above with distilled water. In a 250 ml of conical glass pipette out 50 ml of distilled water and add 5 to 6 drops of potassium chromate as indicator. Now titrate it against standard 0.102 N silver nitrate solution until the formation of reddish-brown precipitate at the end point. Note down the burette reading and repeat the same titration for two more times. Let the constant burette reading be "Y" ml.

13. Questions

1. What is the type of titration used to determine the chloride content?

The chloride content in a water sample is determined by precipitation titration, specifically known as Mohr's method(Argentometric titration).

In this method, a standard solution of silver nitrate (AgNO₃) is used as the titrant.

2. Name the salts that contribute for the chloride content in water.

The chloride content in water mainly comes from soluble chloride salts. The common salts are:

  • Sodium chloride (NaCl)
  • Potassium chloride (KCl)
  • Calcium chloride (CaCl₂)
  • Magnesium chloride (MgCl₂)

3. What are the ill effects of chloride in water?

Chlorides in water are not always harmful in small amounts, but high concentrations can cause several problems:

1. Bad taste:Excess chlorides (especially from sodium chloride and magnesium chloride) give water a salty or unpleasant taste, making it unsuitable for drinking.

2. Corrosion of pipes and boilers:Chloride ions are highly corrosive.They can Damage metal pipes and plumbing, Increase corrosion in boilers and industrial equipment

3. Harmful for plants and soil

4. Health effects (at high levels)

5. Industrial problems

4. Name the indicator used in the above method?

In the chloride determination by Mohr's method, the indicator used is:Potassium chromate (K₂CrO₄)

5. What is a precipitation reaction?

A precipitation reaction is a type of chemical reaction in which two aqueous (water-soluble) solutions react to form an insoluble solid, called a precipitate.

Two soluble salts react → one insoluble product forms as a solid

Example:

When silver nitrate reacts with sodium chloride:

AgNO₃ (aq) + NaCl (aq) → AgCl (↓) + NaNO₃ (aq)

Here, silver chloride (AgCl) is a white precipitate that separates out from the solution.
